# 02-19-2026

The hyper-connectivity of the internet creates the delusion that language barriers are transcendable through technology alone, yet lots gets lost in translation. 

When I started my career in music, I fell victim to this belief. I loved progressive metal, and was one of the 50 Haken fans that made out to a headlining show when they came to town. And so I always played, practiced, and wrote prog metal music while everyone around me was listening to jazz, R&B, and funk. Yet, it confused me why I wasn't ever getting called back to play another gig, or why I was getting kicked out of the bands I was in. Mind you, I also wasn't super good in that genre. But I really enjoyed it. It's just, no one else enjoyed listening to it. I believed that the fan base existed though, and it was just a matter of convincing people around me to also like it. Truthfully, we were just speaking two different languages. I think the internet creates this idea that there is a surplus of people speaking any one language, and that they're all geographically close together, so that if you're playing music of a certain genre, there is an audience. In practice, I've found this to be so far from the truth. Having a shared language in music comes from playing music that is influential in the area, knowing the literature of your area and improving on it. If you're from Athens, GA, you should know the music of the B52s and REM. If you're from Savannah, you should probably know Outkast. I didn't start finding (even minor) success as a musician and a player until I began learning the language the people spoke around me. That said, it just so happens that in America, there is a fairly universal language that almost all musicians speak. That's jazz baby! If you find yourself struggling to play with more people or even connecting to the musicians in your area, first check and make sure you don't need a translator. 

# 02-26-2026

#blog Explaining the more spiritual/Self aspects of singing, counting, and internalizing music without an instrument

When you get deeper into music, theres a recurring emphasis to count your parts out loud, sing them, move with them, etc. As someone who resisted authority without proper explanation, I fought this for a long time. I tended to think that I would show people a different way by blazing ahead, not using these things to slow me down. In other words, I thought I was different and smart enough to be above this. 

Reading books like *The Music Lesson* and *The War of Art* changed this concept for me though, and I hope to break this down enough for others like me to understand. 

It's easy to identify with your instrument. It's even easier to blame your instrument. My struggle with time was for the longest time because I placed the burden of time-keeping responsibility on my set and those around me rather than on myself. The moment I put down my sticks, time became fluid again. I remember encountering this while working at Starbucks one day: we had an alarm going off on the iPads that ran the store. Every time the loop played, I would be off, but I blamed the iPad. Some idiot over at Apple created an imperfect loop just to irritate us musicians. *Smh*. Of course, I was wrong. 

I didn't count when I played. My sense of time had somehow grown "stable" through brute force methods of internalizing the beat and listening to those around me, which years of drumline had drilled into my brain and made me half decent at hearing the pocket without a meteronome. Still, fills and solos would be the moment everyone realized I wasn't actually following time and was just following them. 

What I discovered is that saying "clapping is silly" or "I can't sing" is in itself a form of resistance. You are not your instrument. You are however a human being capable of evolving towards something higher than yourself. I think part of becoming a musician, rather than say a drummer or a guitarist, is realizing that your evolution requires you to dis-identify with the instrument. It requires you to be able to look at music and sing it, play with it, and dance with it without requiring circumstances for its enjoyment. 

Purifying your signal to the Source then becomes a priority, and I think this is where music theory is critical. If you can't hear it, then you won't be able to play it. At least, not very well.

[Ari Hoenig: How to play along with records](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xdSu4RhdAKE) Big takeaways on internalizing the music (Active Listening)
- Stick to one song a week (or two)
- Be able to sing  the melody
- Each song gets min 4 repetitions a day
  1. No play, grasp onto one part to compare with (eg piano, bass, etc)
  2. Play every other chorus
  3. Alternate with the previous
  4. Play full song
- Use each play through to either practice your own coordination exercises, or latch on and practice what they're playing
- Actively compare to what you would play

The more I think about this method, the more genius it seems. It solves so many problems:
- Repeated exposure to the same song until it's completely ingrained
- Repeated exposure to different styles
  - Brushes
  - Mallets
  - Different genres
- Interlocking with different parts
- Soloing 
- Form ingrainment
- Melody and comping exposure
