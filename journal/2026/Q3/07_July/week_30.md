# 07-19-2026

The crazy thing from watching the footage of tonight's show: very little critique. There's parts that are slow, parts where the fills/dynamics can be improved, but overall playing was great. It's even better that the worst thing I can say about my solo is it was just boring.

The main takeaways and things to think about:
- I wrestled a lot with presence during the show. I think I was trying to force this air of creative problem solving, trying to force flow, and instead I just ended up getting stuck thinking about everything. That said, presence habits are becoming more prominent on stage. I found I was much more attentive to what was happening, and there were times I reminded myself to get grounded in the cymbal. And so I ended up being able to feel more of the emotional contents of each song, yet still not sure how to control them AND what I've realized from recording myself is that changing/queuing energy changes needs a lot of work
- It's strange how in a crowd where the musicians will accept you no matter your skill level, I reject them as being not good enough. And as someone who is pursuing perfection, yet is inevitably down on himself for it, I'm unwilling to accept them accepting me

Every idea starts as exciting. But what always happens is somewhere in between
- It's natural and becomes normalized
- It gets disproven and continued attempts generate resistance
For example: the idea of posting my solos by taking the best excerpts and posting them originally felt novel, but now I see that it's unnatural and most people don't care. Hell, I don't even want to watch them. The pain doesn't come from being wrong, but from the weight of what gets put on these ideas. In the solo example, it was more about being able to take excerpts of my practice and post them online and getting famous off them. When it didn't work, it was yet another failure. If possible, it's better to approach each of these ideas not as an end in and of themselves but just as another thing to be tested.
