The hyper-connectivity of the internet creates the delusion that language barriers are transcendable through technology alone, yet lots gets lost in translation. 

When I started my career in music, I fell victim to this belief. I loved progressive metal, and was one of the 50 Haken fans that made out to a headlining show when they came to town. And so I always played, practiced, and wrote prog metal music while everyone around me was listening to jazz, R&B, and funk. Yet, it confused me why I wasn't ever getting called back to play another gig, or why I was getting kicked out of the bands I was in. Mind you, I also wasn't super good in that genre. But I really enjoyed it. It's just, no one else enjoyed listening to it. I believed that the fan base existed though, and it was just a matter of convincing people around me to also like it. Truthfully, we were just speaking two different languages. I think the internet creates this idea that there is a surplus of people speaking any one language, and that they're all geographically close together, so that if you're playing music of a certain genre, there is an audience. In practice, I've found this to be so far from the truth. Having a shared language in music comes from playing music that is influential in the area, knowing the literature of your area and improving on it. If you're from Athens, GA, you should know the music of the B52s and REM. If you're from Savannah, you should probably know Outkast. I didn't start finding (even minor) success as a musician and a player until I began learning the language the people spoke around me. That said, it just so happens that in America, there is a fairly universal language that almost all musicians speak. That's jazz baby! If you find yourself struggling to play with more people or even connecting to the musicians in your area, first check and make sure you don't need a translator.

# 02-21-2026

I think one of the biggest changes in listening to jazz is learning to listen to something in motion. It's akin to meditation in that there's a constant coming and going, a constant letting go, as time flows by. It's meant to be perceived live rather than on record, and it's meant to paint time. Standards are known all across the board as context for a conversation so that each night and each player adds their own color, their own texture, to the greater whole of an ensemble.

The deception comes in *how* you get to a point where you can let go entirely and just listen to the musical conversation. Although it's meant to be almost entirely perceived in the moment, gaining the capacity to listen to it this way requires practicing active listening. It often requires knowing exactly when and where in the chorus of the form you are, down to the beat. In the same way that you want the depth of ingrainment to recall each word when you need it, you should thoughtlessly be able point out where you are and what of the melody line is played, what chord is being played.
