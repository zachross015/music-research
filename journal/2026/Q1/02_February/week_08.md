#blog Explaining the more spiritual/Self aspects of singing, counting, and internalizing music without an instrument

When you get deeper into music, theres a recurring emphasis to count your parts out loud, sing them, move with them, etc. As someone who resisted authority without proper explanation, I fought this for a long time. I tended to think that I would show people a different way by blazing ahead, not using these things to slow me down. In other words, I thought I was different and smart enough to be above this. 

Reading books like *The Music Lesson* and *The War of Art* changed this concept for me though, and I hope to break this down enough for others like me to understand. 

It's easy to identify with your instrument. It's even easier to blame your instrument. My struggle with time was for the longest time because I placed the burden of time-keeping responsibility on my set and those around me rather than on myself. The moment I put down my sticks, time became fluid again. I remember encountering this while working at Starbucks one day: we had an alarm going off on the iPads that ran the store. Every time the loop played, I would be off, but I blamed the iPad. Some idiot over at Apple created an imperfect loop just to irritate us musicians. *Smh*. Of course, I was wrong. 

I didn't count when I played. My sense of time had somehow grown "stable" through brute force methods of internalizing the beat and listening to those around me, which years of drumline had drilled into my brain and made me half decent at hearing the pocket without a meteronome. Still, fills and solos would be the moment everyone realized I wasn't actually following time and was just following them. 

What I discovered is that saying "clapping is silly" or "I can't sing" is in itself a form of resistance. You are not your instrument. You are however a human being capable of evolving towards something higher than yourself. I think part of becoming a musician, rather than say a drummer or a guitarist, is realizing that your evolution requires you to dis-identify with the instrument. It requires you to be able to look at music and sing it, play with it, and dance with it without requiring circumstances for its enjoyment. 

Purifying your signal to the Source then becomes a priority, and I think this is where music theory is critical. If you can't hear it, then you won't be able to play it. At least, not very well.
