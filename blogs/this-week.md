When you get deeper into music, theres an emphasis in counting your parts aloud, singing them, moving with them, etc. As someone who resisted authority without proper explanation, I fought this for a long time. I thought that I could show people a different way by blazing ahead, not being slowed down with the tedium of adopting the status quo into practice. In other words, I thought I was different and smart enough to be above this. 

Reading books like *The Music Lesson* and *The War of Art* changed this concept for me though, and I hope to break this down enough for others like me to understand. 

It's easy identifying with your instrument. My struggle in becoming "heavy" in time was that I placed the burden of time-keeping responsibility on my set and those around me, rather than on myself. The moment I put down my sticks, time became fluid again. I remember encountering this while working at Starbucks one day: we had an alarm going off on the iPads that ran the store. Every time the loop played, I would be off, but I blamed the iPad. Some idiot over at Apple created an imperfect loop just to irritate us musicians. *Smh*. Of course, I was wrong. 

I didn't count when I played. My sense of time had somehow grown "stable" through brute force methods of internalizing the beat and listening to those around me, which years of drumline had drilled into my brain and made me half decent at hearing the pocket without a meteronome. Still, fills and solos would be the moment everyone realized I wasn't actually following time and was just following them. 

Encountering this led me to the realization that when you don't develop the metronomic limb, its sacricifing the skeletal integrity of time for practice speed. It creates an unreliable rhythm player at best, and makes it nearly impossible to intuit things like playing on top of or behind the beat. 

What I discovered is that saying "clapping is silly" or "I can't sing" is in itself a form of resistance. You are not your instrument. You are however a human being capable of evolving towards something higher than yourself. I think part of becoming a musician, rather than say a drummer or a guitarist, is realizing that your evolution requires you to dis-identify with the instrument. It requires you to be able to look at music and sing it, play with it, and dance with it without requiring circumstances for its enjoyment.

Purifying your signal to the Source then becomes a priority, and I think this is where music theory is critical. If you can't hear it, then you won't be able to play it. At least, not very well.

I think the big thing with all of this is just becoming comfortable enough to be a part of music wherever and whenver you are. It shouldnt be confined to a genre or a feeling or a time or a place. It should be allowed everywhere. One of my favorite mentions from *music* by Ted Gioia is the idea that each tree, each leaf, and each rock all make individual sounds. Going out into a forest during a windy day is tanatmount to listening to a symphony but sung in the most natural way possible. Add in bird song and the *ribbit ribbit* of frogs, and you might as well be listening to Mozart himself. 

So how does this all wrap into our original topic? Well it's all about being able to feel music everywhere. Participate in it everywhere. In Yoruban tribes or on the streets of 1900's New Orleans, no cause was needed for music. There was no border between the audience and the participant. The only way to have music was to be a part of it, to let go of your self and participate in the greater community of music. 

The greatest appreciation for music comes from a deeper integration with it. That integration doesn't come on an instrument. What I tend to believe is that there is no integration between the person and their instrument. The only way for us to truly recreate a pitch is to sing it. The only way to truly recreate a rhtym is to clap it. Or to dance to it. We did not start out with instruments, only with our selves. So I guess the way I see it, the mind is in tact with a signal, the universal signal of the Source or God or whatever you want to call it. Through each medium comes a different interpretation, or even distortion of the underlying signal. The closest we can get to being able to interpret this signal is with the voice, clapping, and moving. Having these, being able to truly hear them from within and not without allows us to directly interpret or project their meaning out into the world. 

The way this looks in drumming is different form the other forms: being able to count or hear an internal metornome and sit from that place of focus allows messages to come out into the world with increasing ease. When your practice focus is on maintaining that time while allowing the body to move, then you act as an anchor. Your job is no longer to be impressive or coordinate time so much as it is about maintaining focus on the signal and trusting that the body will communicate what it must into the world.
